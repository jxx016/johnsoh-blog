Most developer outreach is a deck and a booth. You explain the product, hand out stickers, and hope someone integrates it later. I've done a lot of that. It mostly doesn't work.

The stuff that actually moved developers to build looked nothing like a booth. It looked like sitting on the floor of a hackathon house at 11pm debugging someone else's integration. Here's what I learned running outreach at Base's SF hackathon and at EthDenver's Sozu House.

## Show It Working Before You Explain It

At the Base hackathon in SF, I flew in, built a quick demo the night before, and presented it live with our DevRel lead. The thing that landed was not the explanation. It was the moment the demo actually ran and did the thing, on stage, with real output on the screen.

Developers have sat through a thousand pitches. Their default assumption is that your product half-works and the demo is staged. The fastest way past that wall is to run the real thing, live, and let it produce output they didn't expect you to be able to show. Explanation buys you skepticism. A working demo buys you attention.

Lead with the aha. Explain afterward, to the people who are now asking how.

## Build the Demo They're About to Build

At EthDenver, my team needed help presenting for a hackathon, so I built a quick deck and spun out a demo of our GAME product, then presented it to a room of around fifty builders and ran them through how to actually use it.

What worked was that the demo was the same shape as the thing they were about to build that weekend. Not a polished marketing artifact. A scrappy, real integration that looked like their weekend project would look. That does two things: it proves the product works in the messy real case, and it gives them a template. Half of adoption is a developer thinking "oh, that's the pattern, I can copy that."

If your demo is more polished than what a hackathon team can ship in 48 hours, it's the wrong demo. Build down to their reality, not up to your marketing.

## Stay After the Talk

The single highest-leverage thing I did at both events happened after the presentation, not during it. I stayed late to answer questions, unblock integrations, and help people actually get onboarded.

The talk creates interest. The interest dies the moment someone hits their first error and you're not there. Being in the room when a developer hits a `401` or a confusing SDK response, and fixing it with them in two minutes, is worth more than the entire talk that preceded it. That's the moment they go from "interesting" to "it works on my machine," and that moment is the whole game.

Budget more time for the after than the during. The presentation is the ad. The debugging session is the conversion.

## Judge, Don't Just Pitch

At Base I also helped judge the hackathon. Being on the judging side changes what you learn. You see exactly where teams got stuck, which parts of your product they avoided, and what they reached for instead. That's the most honest product feedback you will ever get, because nobody stuck on your SDK at 3am is being polite about it.

If you can get into the judging or mentoring seat at an event, take it. You'll walk out with a prioritized bug list written by the people you most want to keep.

## What Actually Converts

Pulling the threads together, the pattern across both events was the same:

1. **Run the real thing live.** A working demo beats any explanation. Show first, explain second.
2. **Make the demo copyable.** Build at the altitude of a weekend project so it doubles as a template.
3. **Stay for the errors.** The two-minute unblock after the talk is where adoption actually happens.
4. **Get on the judging or mentoring side.** It's the most honest feedback loop you'll find.

None of this scales the way a booth and a deck pretend to. That's the point. Developer adoption isn't won at the top of the funnel with reach. It's won one unblocked integration at a time, in the room, after the talk, when their thing finally runs.
